# Rule ID

MSG-012

# Title

Message delivery semantics

# Category

Events and Messaging

# Status

Active

# Intent

Evaluate whether declared and effective message delivery semantics are known, coherent, and considered by producer and consumer design.

# Applicability

Apply this rule when messages are sent, acknowledged, retried, redelivered, checkpointed, consumed asynchronously, or documented with delivery guarantees.

Do not apply this rule to purely local synchronous calls, message-like structures that are never delivered, or scopes that do not include enough producer, transport, acknowledgement, or consumer behavior to reason about delivery.

# Rule Statement

The architecture must make effective delivery semantics understandable and must design producer and consumer behavior consistently with possible loss, redelivery, acknowledgement timing, timeout, visibility, offset commit, and uncertain failure.

# Rationale

Delivery claims are often local, conditional, or misunderstood. At-most-once, at-least-once, effectively-once, and exactly-once in one component do not automatically describe end-to-end behavior. Incorrect assumptions can cause message loss, repeated effects, stuck processing, or false reliability claims.

# Evidence Required

Evaluation requires evidence of producer send behavior, transport configuration, acknowledgement points, retry and timeout behavior, visibility windows, offset or checkpoint commits, consumer side effects, documentation, tests, and operational behavior.

Primary evidence includes executable producer and consumer flow, broker or queue configuration when available, ack and commit code, error handling, retry paths, integration tests, logs, metrics, and ADRs.

# Evaluation Guidance

Identify the effective delivery semantics across the reviewed flow. Distinguish local broker guarantees from producer-to-consumer outcomes. Evaluate whether the design accounts for redelivery, possible loss, duplicate processing, uncertain commit, or acknowledgement before durable effect.

Common finding shapes include documentation claiming exactly-once end-to-end from a local guarantee, ack before durable processing without accepted loss, offset commit before side effects complete, redelivery ignored by consumers, visibility timeout shorter than processing, and producer send assumptions contradicted by configuration.

Remediation may include clarifying delivery semantics, aligning acknowledgement timing with processing, adjusting consumer assumptions, documenting accepted loss, adding tests for redelivery or loss, or coordinating with idempotency and retry controls. Do not require exactly-once delivery.

Boundaries: `MSG-010` covers producer persistence consistency before handoff; `MSG-013` covers consumer effect idempotency; `MSG-014` covers duplicate handling; `MSG-016` covers retry strategy.

# Pass Conditions

Use `Pass` when evidence shows effective delivery semantics are understood and producer, transport, acknowledgement, checkpoint, retry, and consumer behavior are coherent for the reviewed context.

# Fail Conditions

Use `Fail` when direct evidence shows delivery assumptions are false or ignored in a way that creates message loss, uncontrolled redelivery, incorrect acknowledgement, or misleading end-to-end guarantees.

# Warning Conditions

Use `Warning` when delivery semantics are partially known, locally documented only, inconsistently handled, or weakly evidenced but not proven unsafe.

# Not Applicable Conditions

Use `Not Applicable` when the reviewed scope contains no delivered messages or delivery semantics are outside the selected material and architectural concern.

# Not Enough Evidence Conditions

Use `Not Enough Evidence` when material does not show producer send behavior, transport configuration, acknowledgement, checkpoints, consumer processing, retry, or documentation needed to determine effective delivery.

# Severity Guidance

Severity depends on loss impact, duplicate impact, business criticality, consumer effects, operational detection, reprocessing capability, financial or regulatory risk, and recovery cost.

# Confidence Guidance

Use `Confirmed` when direct evidence shows effective delivery behavior or contradiction. Use `Likely` when several signals support the conclusion with some platform or consumer gaps. Use `Possible` for limited or indirect evidence. Use `Not Enough Evidence` when delivery cannot be traced. Documentation alone must not override behavior.
