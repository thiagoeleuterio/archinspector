# Rule ID

MSG-019

# Title

Correlation and traceability

# Category

Events and Messaging

# Status

Active

# Intent

Evaluate whether asynchronous message flows preserve enough correlation, causation, and trace context to diagnose and reconstruct behavior when needed.

# Applicability

Apply this rule when a business or operational flow spans multiple messages, producers, consumers, retries, reprocessing steps, services, modules, or asynchronous boundaries and diagnosis or reconstruction matters.

Do not apply this rule to isolated messages with no diagnostic need, purely local synchronous work, or general observability concerns unrelated to message-flow correlation.

# Rule Statement

Asynchronous message flows must preserve sufficient context to correlate related messages, understand causality, identify business flow, and diagnose failures without exposing unnecessary sensitive data or excessive cardinality.

# Rationale

Asynchronous communication breaks call stacks and time locality. Without correlation and causation context, operators and reviewers may be unable to reconstruct why a message exists, which business operation caused it, where processing failed, or which downstream effects are related.

This rule is not a generic logging rule and does not require every identifier type simultaneously.

# Evidence Required

Evaluation requires evidence of message identifiers, correlation identifiers, causation identifiers, business identifiers, trace context, envelopes, producer propagation, consumer logging, traces, metrics, audit records, retry context, and documentation.

Primary evidence includes executable propagation code, message envelope definitions, logs with message context, distributed traces, audit records, tests, monitoring configuration, and ADRs.

# Evaluation Guidance

Evaluate whether context is sufficient for the reviewed flow's diagnostic and operational needs. Determine which identifiers are necessary: message ID, correlation ID, causation ID, business ID, trace context, origin, destination, or attempt context. Also evaluate sensitive data and excessive cardinality risks.

Common finding shapes include messages without any usable correlation in multi-step flows, losing trace context at async boundaries, orphan messages in logs, inconsistent propagation across producers, business identifiers missing from diagnostic records, and correlation fields containing sensitive data.

Remediation may include propagating existing trace context, adding correlation or causation identifiers where needed, logging identifiers consistently, preserving origin and destination metadata, using business identifiers carefully, and excluding sensitive payload data. Do not require a specific tracing product.

Boundaries: `MSG-012` evaluates delivery behavior; `MSG-016` retry observability may provide evidence; `MSG-020` replay may use trace evidence but evaluates reprocessing safety.

# Pass Conditions

Use `Pass` when evidence shows message flows carry and preserve context sufficient for correlation, causality, diagnosis, and reconstruction needed by the reviewed context.

# Fail Conditions

Use `Fail` when direct evidence shows async flow context is absent, lost, inconsistent, misleading, or unsafe in a way that prevents required diagnosis or reconstruction.

# Warning Conditions

Use `Warning` when traceability is partial, inconsistent, limited to some hops, missing causality, weakly logged, or potentially exposing sensitive data without proven architectural failure.

# Not Applicable Conditions

Use `Not Applicable` when message-flow correlation is not needed in the reviewed context or no asynchronous/multi-step message flow is present.

# Not Enough Evidence Conditions

Use `Not Enough Evidence` when material does not show message envelopes, identifiers, propagation, logs, traces, consumers, or diagnostic requirements needed to evaluate traceability.

# Severity Guidance

Severity depends on diagnostic criticality, incident impact, flow length, affected consumers, regulatory or security exposure, recovery cost, and ability to reconstruct failures or business causality.

# Confidence Guidance

Use `Confirmed` when direct evidence shows context propagation and diagnostic sufficiency or absence. Use `Likely` when multiple sources align with some gaps. Use `Possible` for limited signals. Use `Not Enough Evidence` when propagation or diagnostic need cannot be established. Generic logging alone must not produce `Confirmed`.
