# Rule ID

MSG-015

# Title

Message ordering

# Category

Events and Messaging

# Status

Active

# Intent

Evaluate whether real ordering requirements are identified and supported at the correct scope without assuming unnecessary global order.

# Applicability

Apply this rule when consumers, projections, business processes, or state transitions rely on message sequence, or when concurrency, partitioning, delayed messages, replay, or retries can affect correctness.

Do not apply this rule when messages are independent, commutative, stateless, or otherwise do not require ordering in the reviewed context.

# Rule Statement

When message order matters, the architecture must identify the required ordering scope and support it at the appropriate level, such as entity, aggregate, business key, partition, stream, or workflow, without assuming broader ordering than the evidence requires.

# Rationale

Messaging systems may not provide global order, and consumers may process concurrently. Incorrect ordering assumptions can make projections stale, reject valid updates, apply old state after new state, or block processing. Excessive ordering requirements can reduce scalability and create unnecessary coupling.

# Evidence Required

Evaluation requires evidence of ordering assumptions, partition or key strategy, sequence numbers, version fields, consumer state transitions, concurrency settings, buffering, rejection, reordering behavior, tests, documentation, and replay paths.

Primary evidence includes handler logic, broker or stream configuration, partition keys, aggregate or entity identifiers, sequence checks, state version checks, delayed-message handling, tests, logs, and ADRs.

# Evaluation Guidance

Determine whether ordering is required and at what scope. Global order, per-entity order, per-aggregate order, per-key order, and per-partition order are different responsibilities. Evaluate delayed and out-of-order messages based on consumer behavior, not assumed transport guarantees.

Common finding shapes include hidden ordering dependence in projections, relying on global order without evidence, missing sequence checks for state overwrites, processing concurrent messages for the same key unsafely, blocking a partition with an old message, and imposing global serialization for independent flows.

Remediation may include documenting ordering scope, choosing an appropriate key, adding sequence or version checks, tolerating late messages, buffering or rejecting stale updates, reducing unnecessary global ordering, or making consumer logic order-insensitive. Do not require specific broker technology.

Boundaries: `MSG-014` evaluates duplicate handling; this rule evaluates order. `MSG-020` evaluates replay safety, which may share sequence evidence but has a different conclusion.

# Pass Conditions

Use `Pass` when evidence shows ordering needs are absent or appropriately identified and supported at the necessary scope without unnecessary global ordering.

# Fail Conditions

Use `Fail` when direct evidence shows required ordering is unsupported or misunderstood, causing real risk from out-of-order, delayed, concurrent, stale, or globally serialized processing.

# Warning Conditions

Use `Warning` when ordering assumptions are implicit, partially supported, weak under concurrency, or overbroad, but evidence of failure or harm is incomplete.

# Not Applicable Conditions

Use `Not Applicable` when messages do not require ordering and consumer behavior is independent of sequence in the reviewed scope.

# Not Enough Evidence Conditions

Use `Not Enough Evidence` when material does not show consumer state dependence, ordering assumptions, keys, concurrency, sequence handling, transport behavior, or tests needed to evaluate order.

# Severity Guidance

Severity depends on incorrect state risk, consumer criticality, number of affected keys or streams, recovery cost, backlog or blocking risk, financial impact, and whether stale events can overwrite newer facts.

# Confidence Guidance

Use `Confirmed` when direct evidence shows ordering requirement and support or violation. Use `Likely` when several signals align with incomplete transport or consumer evidence. Use `Possible` for limited signals. Use `Not Enough Evidence` when order dependence cannot be determined. Topic or partition names alone must not produce `Confirmed`.
