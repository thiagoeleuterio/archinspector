# Rule ID

MSG-017

# Title

Dead-letter handling

# Category

Events and Messaging

# Status

Active

# Intent

Evaluate whether messages that cannot be processed through normal paths are preserved or routed in a way that supports diagnosis, decision, and possible recovery.

# Applicability

Apply this rule when message processing can fail after retries, exceed attempts, be rejected, or require operational resolution outside the normal consumer path.

Do not apply this rule when the reviewed flow cannot produce unprocessed messages, when failure is safely discarded by design with acceptable impact, or when dead-letter behavior is outside the reviewed scope and not required by the selected architecture.

# Rule Statement

When unprocessed messages require operational handling, dead-letter or equivalent handling must preserve enough payload, context, error information, ownership, and retention to support diagnosis, decision, and possible recovery.

# Rationale

A dead-letter queue or store is useful only when it enables action. If failed messages lose context, ownership, origin, destination, attempt history, or retention, operators may be unable to decide whether to discard, repair, replay, or escalate.

# Evidence Required

Evaluation requires evidence of dead-letter routing or equivalent retention, preserved payload, metadata, error context, attempt count, origin, destination, ownership, monitoring, retention, reprocessing, manual resolution, and backlog handling.

Primary evidence includes broker configuration, consumer error handling, failed-message records, logs, metrics, runbooks, reprocessing tools, tests, and ADRs. Queue existence alone is insufficient.

# Evaluation Guidance

Evaluate operational usefulness. Determine what happens after normal processing cannot complete, what context is retained, who owns the backlog, how operators notice it, and whether messages can be resolved according to impact.

Common finding shapes include DLQ existence with no payload or error context, failed messages silently discarded, no owner for backlog, no monitoring, retention too short for recovery needs, reprocessing without safeguards, and dead-letter records missing source or destination.

Remediation may include preserving diagnostic context, documenting ownership, adding monitoring, defining retention, creating controlled reprocessing or discard decisions, and distinguishing recoverable from non-recoverable failures. Do not require dead-letter for every flow or a specific broker feature.

Boundaries: `MSG-016` evaluates retry before exhaustion; this rule evaluates operational handling afterward. `MSG-018` evaluates poison message classification and isolation.

# Pass Conditions

Use `Pass` when evidence shows unprocessed messages that require handling are routed or retained with sufficient context, ownership, monitoring, retention, and recovery or resolution paths.

# Fail Conditions

Use `Fail` when direct evidence shows failed messages are lost, unusable, unowned, unmonitored, prematurely discarded, or impossible to diagnose or resolve despite needing operational handling.

# Warning Conditions

Use `Warning` when dead-letter handling exists but is incomplete, weakly monitored, missing some context, operationally informal, or only partially evidenced.

# Not Applicable Conditions

Use `Not Applicable` when dead-letter or equivalent handling is not required because failures cannot occur, are harmlessly discarded by design, or the reviewed scope has no relevant message processing failure path.

# Not Enough Evidence Conditions

Use `Not Enough Evidence` when material does not show failed-message routing, retained context, ownership, monitoring, retention, reprocessing, or operational procedures.

# Severity Guidance

Severity depends on failed-message criticality, loss impact, backlog growth, recovery need, operational visibility, affected consumers, compliance exposure, and manual correction cost.

# Confidence Guidance

Use `Confirmed` when direct evidence shows dead-letter behavior and operational usefulness or absence. Use `Likely` when several signals align with incomplete operational detail. Use `Possible` for limited signals. Use `Not Enough Evidence` when failed-message handling cannot be inspected. Queue naming alone must not produce `Confirmed`.

# Dependencies

MSG-016, MSG-018.
