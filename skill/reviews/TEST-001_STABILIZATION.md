## 17. Evidence Model Revalidation

The evidence model remains concrete, traceable, and hierarchy-aware. It requires evidence of the declared mechanism, architectural characteristic, objective, protected property, verification criterion, produced result, and intended interpretation.

Evidence remains separated from conclusions. Naming, folders, labels, comments, and conventions remain weak auxiliary signals and cannot by themselves support `Confirmed` confidence.

## 18. Strong Evidence Revalidation

Strong evidence remains, in order:

1. executable or repeatable implementation of the fitness function;
2. explicit definition of the architectural characteristic;
3. evaluation criterion;
4. produced or observable result;
5. condition of conformance;
6. condition of violation;
7. positive verification cases;
8. negative verification cases;
9. tests or checks of the verification mechanism itself;
10. historical execution records;
11. execution configuration.

Strong evidence can support `Pass` or `Fail` only when applicability, architectural meaning, criterion, result, and interpretation are sufficiently established.

## 19. Weak Evidence Revalidation

Weak evidence remains limited to supporting status:

- documentation of architectural intent;
- ADR or related decision record;
- names;
- folders;
- comments;
- labels;
- conventions;
- installed tools;
- isolated pipeline labels.

Weak evidence does not independently sustain `Pass`, `Fail`, or `Confirmed` confidence when the mechanism, protected property, criterion, result, or interpretation is missing.

## 20. Outcome Revalidation

`TEST-001` retains exactly five official outcomes: `Pass`, `Fail`, `Warning`, `Not Applicable`, and `Not Enough Evidence`.

No outcome is missing, duplicated, renamed, or supplemented by a sixth outcome. No outcome is automatically mapped to confidence or severity.

### Pass

`Pass` requires sufficient evidence that a declared fitness function or equivalent architectural verification mechanism protects a real architectural characteristic, has an explicit objective, identifies the protected property, uses a verifiable criterion, and produces an interpretable architectural result.

Manual, qualitative, dynamic, static, local, operational, or document-supported mechanisms can pass when they are repeatable enough for the reviewed context and their architectural interpretation is clear.

### Fail

`Fail` requires confirmed applicability, sufficient evidence, a declared or alleged fitness function or equivalent architectural control, and a real problem in the central responsibility of this Rule.

The Rule does not fail a system merely because there is no automation, pipeline, tool, ADR, threshold, architecture-testing library, source-code implementation, execution on every commit, static test, or preferred technology.

### Warning

`Warning` indicates a plausible fitness function or architectural control whose definition is partial, inconsistent, fragile, or ambiguous, without enough basis for definitive `Fail`.

`Warning` is not a substitute for `Not Enough Evidence`.

### Not Applicable

`Not Applicable` indicates legitimate absence of a declared fitness function, alleged architectural verification mechanism, architecture control claim, or relevant scope.

It does not approve the architecture globally and does not mean architecture drift is absent.

### Not Enough Evidence

`Not Enough Evidence` indicates that the Rule may be relevant but reviewed material cannot establish the mechanism, architectural characteristic, objective, protected property, criterion, result, interpretation, or scope sufficiently for a reliable conclusion.

Absence of evidence is not converted into `Fail`.

## 21. Confidence Revalidation

Confidence remains evidence-based and independent from outcome and severity.

- `Confirmed` requires direct, traceable evidence establishing mechanism, characteristic, objective, protected property, criterion, result, and interpretation.
- `Likely` requires multiple consistent evidence points with some incomplete direct confirmation.
- `Possible` applies to limited, indirect, naming-based with partial corroboration, or structurally suggestive evidence.
- `Not Enough Evidence` applies when material cannot support a useful confidence level.

The Rule uses no score, percentage, formula, or automatic confidence mapping. Naming alone cannot produce `Confirmed`.

## 22. Severity Revalidation

Severity remains contextual and impact-based.

The Rule considers criticality of the protected characteristic, risk of the decision, false coverage, breadth, affected modules or services, recurrence, regressions, recovery cost, operational risk, security, regulatory exposure, financial exposure, alternative controls, organizational dependence, and false sense of conformance.

No fixed severity, score, formula, threshold, or automatic mapping from outcome to severity is used.
